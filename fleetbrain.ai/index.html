The Bodewell Doctrine

Control in Plain English

Operators don't need to write code. They read the agent's output and say yes or no. When something is wrong, they describe the correction in plain English. That correction becomes a permanent rule the agent follows from that point forward.

Instructions are natural language, not configuration files. There is no YAML, no JSON, no scripting. The control bus is human-readable because the humans using it are business operators, not engineers.

Think of it as rubber bands on the claws. The agent can work. It can research, draft, analyze, and recommend. But it cannot reach the outside world without a human in the loop. The safety controls are designed to let the agent be productive while preventing harm.

This is not a philosophical position. It is an engineering decision. Unsupervised AI agents are in the news every week for sending bad emails, making unauthorized commitments, and leaking data. FleetBrain is the managed alternative.

Case Study

Jeff Fries, RapidKey Locksmith

A real engagement, including the mistakes.

DeSoto, Kansas

A $2,500 Equipment Decision

Jeff Fries runs RapidKey Locksmith and was facing a decision on automotive locksmith equipment. The full package was $2,500. He wasn't sure it was the right move for his market.

He messaged Stan, the FleetBrain AI agent, with a straightforward question. Stan ran live market research, identifying the top 3 vehicles in the KC metro area to determine which tools would get the most use. He built a business decision framework comparing a $1,200 starter approach against the $3,100 full commitment, with defined checkpoints for scaling up.

Stan then opened three vendor websites, compared pricing, stock availability, reviews, and shipping costs. He identified $267 in savings through vendor comparison and recommended a $599-800 starter pack backed by actual market data.

~10 min Research Time
$267 Savings Found
2+ hrs Jeff's Estimate to DIY

Jeff placed the order based on Stan's recommendation. This wasn't a demo or a staged scenario. Jeff messaged Stan with a real question while the operator was out shopping.

The system made mistakes during this engagement. Stan recommended a vendor before checking reviews and used a sales-pitch tone Jeff hadn't asked for. The operator caught both errors and issued permanent corrections on the spot. Those corrections now apply to every future engagement. That's the loop.
